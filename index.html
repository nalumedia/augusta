How the math works. Vegas outright odds are de-vigged into implied win probabilities, then converted into per-player expected 72-hole scores via μᵢ = 293 − scale · ln(pᵢ / p_uniform). Each simulation draws a score from Normal(μᵢ, σ), with probability 1−P(make cut) of triggering the missed-cut penalty instead. For your 6 picks, we sort the 6 scores and sum the lowest 4. Repeat 10,000 times to get the expected total and distribution. Default calibration reproduces Scheffler's book win probability (~12%) within 0.5%.

What Actually Matters at Augusta

Roughly ranked by signal strength. The first four drive most of the Augusta-adjustment math above — the rest are noise once baseline skill is priced in.
#1 · Strongest

SG: Approach (last 12 weeks)

The single biggest predictor at Augusta by a wide margin. The greens are so severe that iron play into the correct quadrants matters more than anywhere else on tour. Scheffler, Rahm, DeChambeau, Morikawa all elite here — and it shows in the gold SG:APP chips on their player rows.

#2

Driving: distance + accuracy combo

Augusta rewards distance — four par-5s you want to reach in two — and punishes crooked drives on 10, 11, 13, and 18. Pure bombers without control (Finau, Niemann historically) underperform their odds. Flip the Weather toggle to "Soft / Calm" and watch the bombers' projected scores plummet.

#3

Augusta history / Course fit

Past top-10s at Augusta are genuinely predictive — real "horses for courses" signal. Couples, Mickelson, Garcia outperformed their baseline skill at Augusta for years. DataGolf's course-fit adjustment moves projections by ~1–2 strokes for veterans. That's exactly what the Δ column and the Course-fit toggle above encode.

#4

Short game / SG: ARG

Augusta's runoffs mean you miss greens in scary spots. Elite scramblers (Spieth, peak Mickelson, Cameron Smith) punch above their weight because they save pars from places most of the field bogies. Bundled into the course-fit delta for known wizards.

Moderate

Age curve

Masters winners skew 28–36. Under-25 is rare (Spieth '15, Woods '97); over-45 is very rare post-Mickelson '21. Augusta rewards experience but punishes decline. Modeled as a small +0.3 bump at the edges.

Moderate

LIV rust

LIV players face real rust and competitive-reps problems at majors — 54 holes with no cut isn't great prep. The gap is maybe 0.3–0.5 strokes/round of hidden penalty vs. their pre-LIV baseline. Toggle it on and Rahm/DeChambeau/Koepka/DJ slide a bit.

Weak / Noisy

Weather

Tournament-wide weather matters — wind disperses the field, soft conditions help bombers — but it's hard to forecast on Monday and it affects everyone. Use the scenario toggle to see how conditions tilt the field ±2–3 strokes before locking picks.

Don't double-count

Putting · World Ranking · Nationality

Putting is the noisiest stat week-to-week and Augusta's greens don't transfer cleanly from regular tour numbers. World Ranking is already baked into Vegas odds — don't count it twice. Nationality is ~zero signal once you control for skill.

How to Use / FAQ

Getting Started

How do I pick a team?

Click any player card on the left to add them to your 6. Click again to remove. The counter at the top of the pool shows how many you've selected — the right side updates live once you have 6.

What's the "Find Optimal" button?

It brute-forces every combination of 6 from the top 18 players and picks the combo with the lowest expected best-4 total. This is the model's single best answer — usually Scheffler + Rahm + DeChambeau + McIlroy + Schauffele + Åberg.

What are the presets?

Curated "theme" combos — Chalk, LIV All-Stars, Past Champions, etc. Each shows the historical best-4 average across years where all 6 played, so you can compare narratives against actual prior-Masters performance.

Search and filter?

Type a name in the search box to filter the pool. Toggle the tour chips (PGA / LIV / INT) to narrow down. Your selections stay put regardless of filter.

What the Numbers Mean

Expected Best-4 Total

The headline number. It's the average across 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations of the sum of your 4 lowest scores (out of your 6 picks). Lower is better.

Par for 4 rounds at Augusta is 288, so 4 × 288 = 1152 is "all four picks shot par." The "vs par" number tells you how many strokes under (or over) that benchmark your team averages.

σ (sigma)

Standard deviation of your best-4 total across sims. A small σ means your team is consistent; a big σ means boom-or-bust. LIV-heavy teams tend to have higher σ.

P10 / P90

The 10th and 90th percentile outcomes. P10 is your "things broke right" score (beat it 10% of the time), P90 is your "things went sideways" score. The gap between them is your team's realistic range.

Per-player projected score

Each player's mean 72-hole score from the skill model, calibrated so the implied win probabilities match Vegas. Scheffler's ~281 means the model thinks he averages ~−7 at Augusta.

Cut% and Win%

Cut% is the chance that player misses the cut (triggers the penalty). Win% is their modeled chance of posting the outright lowest score of the field in a single sim — roughly matches the de-vigged book price.

Histogram

The distribution of your team's best-4 totals across all 10k sims. A tall narrow peak = consistent team. A long left tail = ceiling upside. The red line marks the mean.

Historical Backtest

How does it work?

For each of the last 7 Masters, we take your 6 picks, grab their actual scores that year, and sum the lowest 4. That's what your team would have scored. If fewer than 4 of your picks played a given year, it's marked N/A.

What do the chips mean?

Green chips = one of the 4 scores that counted for you. Gray = played but was one of the 2 dropped scores. Red dashed = missed cut or WD (score was treated as a penalty). "—" = didn't play that year at all (DNP).

Why the summary line?

Shows your team's historical average best-4 across qualifying years, plus the year-level best/worst. Use it as a sanity check on presets — e.g. LIV All-Stars has the single best backtest year (2020, −44) but is wildly inconsistent.

Why does the optimal pick often trail Past Champions historically?

Because the optimal pick is forward-looking — it's betting on 2026 form, Vegas odds, and current skill. "Past Champions" has survivorship bias baked in: it's picking players we already know performed at Augusta. The model doesn't get to cheat with hindsight.

Model Parameters

Skill scale

Controls how much better the favorites are than the field. Higher values spread out projected scores — Scheffler gets lower, longshots get higher. Default 2.0 reproduces the book's win distribution.

σ (per-round noise)

How much a single player's score bounces around their mean. Default 4.5 strokes matches historical PGA Tour variance at majors. Bumping it up makes the tournament more random and helps longshots.

Missed-cut penalty

The score assigned when a sim says a player missed the cut. Default 310 (≈ +22) is deliberately brutal — a player who misses the cut is effectively unusable in best-4 because you're relying on their teammates to absorb the loss.

Number of sims

10,000 Monte Carlo iterations per refresh. Standard error on the expected total is roughly σ/√10000 ≈ 0.1 strokes, so the headline number is precise to about a tenth.

TL;DR — Pick 6 players. The 4 lowest scores count. The tool tells you (a) what the model expects you to shoot, (b) how that team would've done in prior Masters, and (c) how your choice compares to the mathematically optimal pick. Lower numbers win.
